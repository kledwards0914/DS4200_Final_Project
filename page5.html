Conclusion

What did we learn from our visualizations?

First, genre influences popularity, but it does not determine it. The boxplot showed wide variance within genres, suggesting that audio features within a genre matter more than the genre label alone. Second, most songs across genres fall into the low popularity tier, but genres like dubstep, folk, and mandopop show a more balanced split between medium and high popularity, suggesting those genres produce more consistently well-received tracks. Third, tempo alone does not determine danceability. The scatter plot showed that danceability varies widely even at similar tempos depending on genre, with some genres clustering at high danceability regardless of BPM. Fourth, most genres skew toward high energy, and the linked chart shows that higher energy ranges don't necessarily correspond to higher average popularity; the relationship is more nuanced than it appears. Finally, the artist bar chart revealed that even well-known artists like Ella Fitzgerald and The Beatles only appear around 10 times in the dataset, highlighting how spread out the data is across a massive range of artists. For future work, it would be interesting to bring in time based data, seeing as how these features have shifted over decades, and whether what counts as 'popular' has changed. We'd also want to incorporate artist follower counts, since some research suggests that artist following is one of the strongest predictors of popularity. Thanks for visiting our website!
