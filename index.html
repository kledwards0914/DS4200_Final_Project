Introduction:

Hi everyone we're Kaitlyn, Mia, and Isabelle, and our project is called Exploring the Relationship Between Audio Features and Popularity in Spotify Music. Music streaming platforms like Spotify use audio signals to power their recommendation algorithms. We were curious about what actually makes a song popular. Whether that’s how danceable it is, how much energy it has, or whether it's explicit. These questions are what drove our project. Our main tasks for designing our visuals included: first, understanding how popularity varies across genres; second, exploring how explicit content is distributed across genres; third, examining the relationship between tempo and danceability for top artists; fourth, seeing how energy levels differ across the most common genres. To do this, we used a dataset from Kaggle containing over 114,000 Spotify tracks spanning 125 genres, with audio features like danceability, energy, tempo, loudness, and more, along with metadata like genre, artist, and whether a song is explicit.

Conclusion: What did we learn from our visualizations?

First, genre influences popularity, but it does not determine it. The boxplot we created showed wide variance within genre, which suggests that specific audio features within a genre matter more than the genre label alone. Second, we discovered explicit content is based on a song's words, not its sound or energy level. The Sankey Diagram showed EDM and house tend to be clean despite being high energy, while hip-hop and metal genres dominate the explicit side. Third, high energy is nearly universal, but differs in degree. The histogram showed almost all top genres are concentrated in the 0.75-1.0 range, with black-metal dominating the very top. Fourth, tempo alone does not determine how danceable a song is. The scatter plot showed that honky-tonk songs are highly danceable regardless of tempo, while heavy metal songs score low even at similar tempos. This suggests that genre style and rhythm matter more than speed. For future work, it would be interesting to bring in time based data, seeing as how these features have shifted over decades, and whether what counts as 'popular' has changed. We'd also want to incorporate artist follower counts, since some research suggests that artist following is one of the strongest predictors of popularity. Thanks for visiting our website!
